Introduction¶

Provide an introduction to your analysis. (minimum 5 sentences) Your director has tasked you with analyzing the impact of photo content. In short, your company has long operated under the general idea that photos are the most engaging form of social media content, and that they should advise their clients to post as many of these as they can.

The objective of this project was to analyze a dataset of Facebook posts and their engagement metrics, through principal component analysis, KMeans clustering, and finally a binary classification task.

I found that 4 principal components represent the underlying data:

  • Highly Engaging
  • Positive and Surprising
  • Outrageous
  • Emotionally Polarizing

I also found that the PCA-based data can be clustered well into 5 segments:

  • Not Engaging 🥱
  • Angry Outliers 😡
  • Positive and Surprising 🤩
  • Super Engaging 😁
  • Sad Outliers 😭

I also analyzed the status types of each segment and discovered that photos tend to have fewer highly engaging posts compared to video, and have several specific outliers that elicit many angry and sad reactions.

Lastly, I compared several logistic regression models and found that the model using the "Retained Principal Component" features was best because it had good test performance and, notably, the smallest train-test gap of all the models.

First, one initial observation is that the data contains mostly photos (1443 examples), as well as a large number of videos (763) and a small number of text posts (156).

In terms of reactions (likes, loves, etc.), photos appear to perform the worst compared to videos and text content. The mean of reactions in each category for photos is lower than that for videos and text, with the exceptions of 'num_loves' and 'num_angrys,' where the mean for photos slightly outperforms that of text posts.

According to Julian Thompson (2013), total engagement on social media is defined as a comprehensive metric that combines both active and passive user interactions with content. Active interactions, such as likes, comments, and shares, amplify content visibility through networks of friends and can lead to viral spread. On the other hand, passive interactions include actions like post clicks, link clicks, and image views, which indicate content consumption without further dissemination. Within this framework of total engagement, the provided metrics show that photos are not the leading format. Instead, videos tend to attract a broader range of active reactions, indicating higher overall engagement, while text posts consistently garner the most likes.

In relation to specific reactions, photos seem to have a balanced but modest engagement profile. They do not lead in eliciting strong reactions compared to videos and text, but as mentioned above, they do have the edge over text in terms of 'loves' and 'angrys'. For likes, photos hold their ground with an average of 175.45, trailing behind text posts yet still demonstrating a decent level of engagement. Turning to 'wows', the mean for photos is 0.68, hinting that they can at times deliver content that is unexpectedly impactful, albeit less consistently than videos. Regarding 'hahas', photos generate a light-hearted response with a mean of 0.12, which, while lower than videos, signifies their ability to amuse. With a mean of 0.17 for 'sads', photos occasionally touch on more somber emotions, though less prominently than other content types.

# Helper functions

def scree_plot(pca_object, vertical_line_xloc=None):
    """
    Visualizes a scree plot from a pca object.

    If vertical_line_xloc is passed in, draws a vertical line at that x location.
    """
    # building a scree plot

    # setting plot size
    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 8))
    features = range(pca_object.n_components_)


    # developing a scree plot
    plt.plot(features,
             pca_object.explained_variance_ratio_,
             linewidth = 2,
             marker = 'o',
             markersize = 10,
             markeredgecolor = 'black',
             markerfacecolor = 'grey')


    # setting more plot options
    plt.title('Scree Plot')
    plt.xlabel('PCA feature')
    plt.ylabel('Explained Variance')
    plt.xticks(features)

    if vertical_line_xloc:
        plt.axvline(vertical_line_xloc, linestyle='--', color='red')
        
    # displaying the plot
    plt.show()

def scaler(df):
    """
    Standardizes a dataset (mean = 0, variance = 1). Returns a new DataFrame.
    Requires sklearn.preprocessing.StandardScaler()
    
    PARAMETERS
    ----------
    df     | DataFrame to be used for scaling
    """

    # INSTANTIATING a StandardScaler() object
    scaler = StandardScaler(copy = True)


    # FITTING the scaler with the data
    scaler.fit(df)


    # TRANSFORMING our data after fit
    x_scaled = scaler.transform(df)

    
    # converting scaled data into a DataFrame
    new_df = pd.DataFrame(x_scaled)


    # reattaching column names
    new_df.columns = list(df.columns)
    
    return new_df

Develop a scree plot and determine how many principal components you would like to retain. Draw a vertical line in the scree plot to indicate your cutoff point. Explain your rationale. (minimum 5 sentences)

Based on the Scree plot without any constraints on the number of features, I've determined that four principal components will be optimal for further analysis. This decision is based on two key considerations:

  1. The Elbow Criterion: the concept of the 'elbow' is crucial in determining the number of components or clusters to retain. According to Ghiasian & Lewis (2020), this method involves identifying the point in the graph where the explained variance starts to diminish, suggesting that additional components offer diminished returns. In our Scree plot, the elbow appears to occur at the fourth component, indicating it as a natural choice for cutoff.

  2. Variance Explained: the primary objective of Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is to condense the dataset into a smaller set of features without significantly sacrificing information. A standard rule of thumb, as discussed in class, is to select components that account for at least 80% of the variance. While the four components I've selected account for approximately 75% of the variance—as further detailed analysis indicates, precisely 76%—this is reasonably close to the desired threshold, ensuring I capture the bulk of the dataset's information while simplifying its complexity.

In conclusion, I selected four features based on these criteria: the presence of an elbow in the Scree plot and the components' combined contribution to a substantial portion of the dataset's variance, close to the 80% benchmark.

1. Principal Component 0 (PC0) - Highly Engaging¶

PC0 shows a profile in which all variables exhibit a positive correlation, although to varying degrees. This suggests that PC0 captures overall engagement of a post.

Specifically:

  • Strongest Positive Correlation: 'num_loves' (0.50) holds the most substantial positive correlation with PC0, indicating that expressions of love are a pivotal element of this component. The 'Love' Reaction signifies a deep appreciation for the content, suggesting that the post has meaningfully connected with the audience. It can also denote strong support for a person or cause, showing that the post has touched the audience's heart in some significant way. This powerful correlation implies that PC0 might represent content that not only garners approval but also deeply resonates and evokes a heartfelt response from viewers, encompassing a full spectrum of positive emotional engagement.
  • Strong Positive correlations: Other reactions such as 'num_shares' (0.49), 'num_comments' (0.41), 'num_hahas' (0.37), and 'num_wows' (0.30) also show strong positive correlations, suggesting that this component may represent a mix of content appreciation, humor, and surprise-aspects that actively engage users and encourage them to share their thoughts and spread the content.
  • Weaker Correlations: 'num_likes' (0.16) demonstrate a positive but relatively modest influence on PC0, suggesting that 'likes' contribute to engagement but may not fully capture the emotional resonance of content in the way 'loves' do. On the other hand, "num_sads" (0.20) and "num_angrys" (0.22) also show positive correlations, albeit weaker, but carry significant weight in terms of emotional depth and complexity. The "sad" reaction can sometimes symbolize empathy and shared grief conveyed by the video; its positive correlation indicates that PC0 recognizes content that deeply moves the audience in a shared grief experience. Similarly, the reaction "angry" is used to denote disapproval and strong dissatisfaction, allowing users to express their discontent in a constructive way. Its correlation with PC0 emphasizes that engagement is not only about positive statements, but also about content that can critically engages and elicits strong responses.

These responses underscore that PC0 measures not only liking or loving the content, but also captures the range of emotions that the content elicits, including those of comfort, empathy, and shared dissent. In short, PC0 represents engaging content.

2. Principal Component 1 (PC1) - Positive and Surprising¶

PC1 is mostly represented by positive association with num_likes and num_wows and negative association from num_comments and num_sads. Overall, PC1 reprensents positive and surprising content.

  • Strongest Positive Correlation: 'num_likes' (0.73) is strongly positively correlated with PC1.The traditional “Like” button on Facebook has been around since the platform’s inception. It was initially designed as a way for users to show their approval or agreement with a post or comment. However, over time, the meaning of the “Like” button has evolved.

“Like” button can signify various things depending on the context. It can indicate agreement, support, appreciation, or simply acknowledging that you have seen the post (BrandDad Social, 2023). It is a versatile tool that allows users to engage with content in a quick and straightforward manner. This suggests that PC1 may correspond to content that is broadly appealing and receives a high volume of positive acknowledgment from the audience.

  • Strong Positive Correlation: 'num_wows' (0.48) also has a positive correlation, suggesting that PC1 reflects content that sometimes surprises or impresses the audience.
  • No Correlation: 'num_loves' (0.00) and 'num_hahas' (0.01) show almost no correlation with PC1, which means that expressions of love and amusement do not significantly define this component. This could imply that the 'likes' and 'wows' are more indicative of content that is appreciated on a more casual or aesthetic level, rather than content that creates a strong emotional bond or comedic value.
  • Negative Correlations: 'num_comments' (-0.31), 'num_sads' (-0.35), 'num_shares' (-0.12) and 'num_angrys' (-0.04) are negatively correlated with PC1. This suggests that content associated with this component tends to generate less discussion (fewer comments) and shares and is less likely to evoke feelings of sadness or anger. The negative correlation with comments could indicate that while the content is liked, it may not inspire extensive conversations or debates.

3. Principal Component 2 (PC2) - Outrageous¶

PC2 is most positively associated with num_angrys and negatively associated with num_likes and num_sads. It's worth noting that the dataset contains very few num_angry reactions (the 75% is 0 num_angry), so this field should be considered more of an outlier. However, still, because of the primary association with num_angrys and negative with num_likes and num_sad, PC2 overall represents outrageous content.

  • Strongest Correlations, Positive and Negative: The most notable feature of PC2 is its strong positive correlation with 'num_angrys' (0.63) and a strong negative correlation with 'num_sads'(-0.68). This indicates that PC2 may represent posts that provoke a response of anger. It seems to capture the type of engagement where users are moved to express more confrontational or reactive emotions (anger) as opposed to more contemplative or empathetic emotions (sadness).
  • Strong Negative Correlation : 'num_likes' (-0.35)
  • Neutral to Weak Correlations: 'num_shares' (0.05), 'num_loves' (0.06), 'num_wows' (0.08), 'num_comments' (- 0.03),and 'num_hahas' (-0.05) show neutral to weak correlations with PC2.

4. Principal Component 3 (PC3) - Emotionally Polarizing¶

PC3 is most positively correlated with num_angrys and num_sads, and with some positive correlation with num_likes, which overall suggests it's emotionally polarizing content (diving reactions between positive and negative responses). This might represent content that deals with challenging or controversial topics that resonate deeply and personally with the audience, triggering these potent responses. The negative correlations with 'loves' and 'wows' may indicate that while the content is engaging, it may not be enjoyable in a conventional sense, but rather important, thought-provoking, or even upsetting

  • Strongest Correlation: 'num_angrys' (0.72) shows the highest positive correlation, suggesting that PC3 may capture content that is most likely to provoke anger or frustration.
  • Notable Positive Correlation: 'num_sads' (0.54) is also significantly correlated, indicating that this component represents content that can evoke feelings of sadness or empathy.
  • Negative Correlations: 'num_loves' (-0.08) and 'num_wows' (-0.08) have slight negative correlations with PC3, suggesting that the content related to this component is less likely to inspire feelings of affection or surprise.
  • Weaker Correlations: 'num_likes' (0.25) shows a moderate positive correlation, while 'num_comments' (-0.20), 'num_shares' (-0.17), and 'num_hahas' (-0.20) are negatively correlated. This pattern implies that PC3 is not predominantly about the amount of engagement (likes, comments, shares) or humor.

Why 5 clusters?¶

Decide on an ideal number of clusters and explain your rationale. (minimum 5 sentences)

I chose 5 clusters for several main reasons:

  1. The intertia plot has a clear elbow around k=5. Inertia represents roughly how well a KMeans model fits the data. As expected, k=1 does not fit the data that well (it's just one large group), and the inertia goes down with every increment in K. Also as expected, with a large number of K, the marginal benefit decreases, because if you have 9 clusters than having 10 will likely only fit the data slightly better. Observing the plot, it is clear that k=5 strikes an appropriate balance between having an efficient representation and sufficient inertia.
  2. The dendrogram validates that there are roughly 4-6 main clusters (~two red, two green, one orange in the plot above). This further indicates that 5 is a reasonable choice.
  3. Avoiding overfitting. I decided not to chose a larger number because it would risk overfitting the data, and prevent the KMeans model from being as helpful to generalize to new data.
  4. Lastly, it’s important for me that each cluster has a meaningful interpretation and that the model performance is robust. The five-cluster solution appears to provide a solid balance between detail and generalizability, which is why I believe it’s the ideal number of clusters for my analysis.

True Positive and True Negatives represent examples that the model predicted correctly, and thus are not errors and do not pose a risk to the business.

However, the 144 False Positives and 12 False Negatives are all errors, and do represent risks for the business. Generally speaking, a False Positive represents when the model incorrectly makes a positive prediction (when the true value is negative) and the risk would be when a negative example being considered as a positive (e.g. in a spam filter, a valid email may be incorrectly flagged as spam, which is why sometimes people as you to check your spam folder).

Conversely, a False Negative represents when the model incorrectly makes a negative prediction (when the true value is positive), and the risk would be considering a positive value as a negative. In the case of the spam filter, this could mean a spam email gets through to your inbox (which happens sometimes).

As a reminder of the stated machine learning problem here, the input is a list of features about a post and the output is whether said post is a photo post, or a video/text post. This may not necessarily seem like an important problem to solve on first glance, but the premise is that a Vietnamese marketing firm is looking to gain insights about posts and this prediction task can provide important insights for the business and the advice it shares with clients about how to make the most engaging posts.

The company has long operated under the idea that photos are the most engaging forms of content, and that they should advice their clients to post as many of them as they can -- I have already invalidated this hypothesis partly from the initial analysis at the start of the report.

However, now that I've built an ML model that can predict whether a post is a photo or non-photo post, this could potentially be helpful to glean insights into what constitutes a photo post.

So, the associated risk to the marketing business for a False Negative is that they may think a photo could be text or video when it's actually a photo. And for a False Positive, they may think a photo post is actually a text/video post. Both of these could confuse them and decrease the quality of their recommendations to clients if they rely on these models to understand post types.
